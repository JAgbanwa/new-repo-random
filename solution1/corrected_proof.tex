\begin{theorem}
Let $\tau(n)$ denote the number of positive divisors of $n$.  No integer $n > 24$
satisfies
\[
  \max_{1 \le m < n}\bigl(m + \tau(m)\bigr) \le n + 2.
\]
Moreover, $n = 24$ is the largest positive integer satisfying this inequality.
\end{theorem}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:even-tau4}
Let $N > 8$ be even.  If $\tau(N) \le 4$, then $N = 2p$ for some prime $p$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write $N = 2^a m$ with $m$ odd and $a \ge 1$.  Then $\tau(N) = (a+1)\tau(m) \le 4$.

If $a \ge 2$ then $a + 1 \ge 3$, forcing $\tau(m) = 1$, i.e.\ $m = 1$ and
$N = 2^a$.  But $N > 8$ gives $a \ge 4$ and $\tau(N) = a + 1 \ge 5$, a
contradiction.

Hence $a = 1$, $N = 2m$, $2\tau(m) \le 4$, so $\tau(m) \le 2$.  Since $m$ is
odd, $\tau(m) = 1$ gives $m = 1$ and $N = 2 \le 8$, excluded.  Thus $\tau(m) = 2$,
$m$ is an odd prime $p$, and $N = 2p$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem]
Suppose for contradiction that $n > 24$ satisfies the inequality.
Setting $m = n - k$ yields the key constraint:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:star}
  \tau(n-k) \le k + 2 \qquad \text{for all } 1 \le k < n. \tag{$*$}
\end{equation}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 1: $n$ is even.}

Suppose $n$ is odd.  Then $n - 1$ is even and $n - 1 > 8$.  By~\eqref{eq:star}
with $k = 1$ we have $\tau(n-1) \le 3$.  Since $n - 1$ is even and greater
than $2$, it is not prime, so $\tau(n-1) \ne 2$.  If $\tau(n-1) = 3$ then
$n - 1 = q^2$ for some prime $q$; but $q^2$ is odd only if $q$ is odd, and
then $n = q^2 + 1$ is even, contradicting $n$ odd.  Thus $\tau(n - 1) = 1$,
i.e.\ $n - 1 = 1$, which is impossible for $n > 24$.  Hence $n$ is even.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 2: $n - 2 = 2q$ for a prime $q$.}

Since $n$ is even and $n > 24$, we have $n - 2 > 8$ even.  By~\eqref{eq:star}
with $k = 2$: $\tau(n-2) \le 4$.  Lemma~\ref{lem:even-tau4} gives $n - 2 = 2q$
for a prime $q$, so $n = 2q + 2$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 3: $\tau(n-1) \le 3$ and two cases.}

Since $n - 1 = 2q + 1$ is odd, by~\eqref{eq:star} with $k = 1$ we have
$\tau(n-1) \le 3$.  An odd integer greater than $1$ with $\tau \le 3$ is either
prime ($\tau = 2$) or the square of a prime ($\tau = 3$).

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 1: $n - 1 = p^2$ for a prime $p$.}

Then $n - 2 = p^2 - 1 = (p-1)(p+1)$.  Since $n > 24$ we have $p^2 > 23$, so
$p \ge 5$, which gives $p - 1 \ge 4$.  Because $p$ is an odd prime, both
$p - 1$ and $p + 1$ are even, so $2 \mid p^2 - 1$.  Hence the following are
distinct divisors of $n - 2$:
\[
  1,\quad 2,\quad p-1,\quad p+1,\quad p^2 - 1.
\]
(They are distinct because $1 < 2 < p - 1 < p + 1 < p^2 - 1$ for $p \ge 5$.)
Thus $\tau(n-2) \ge 5 > 4$, contradicting~\eqref{eq:star} with $k = 2$.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Case 2: $n - 1 = p$ is prime.}

Then $p = 2q + 1$, so $p$ and $q$ are both prime and $p = 2q + 1$ is a Sophie
Germain pair.  Since $n = 2q + 2 > 24$ we have $q \ge 13$.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 2a: $q = 2r + 1$ for a prime $r$.}

By~\eqref{eq:star} with $k = 4$: $\tau(n - 4) \le 6$.  Now
$n - 4 = 2q - 2 = 2(q - 1)$.  Write $q - 1 = 2^a r$ with $r$ odd, $a \ge 1$;
then $n - 4 = 2^{a+1} r$ and $\tau(n-4) = (a+2)\tau(r) \le 6$.  We
examine all cases:

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{$a \ge 2$, $r > 1$:} $\tau(n-4) \ge (a+2)\cdot 2 \ge 4 \cdot 2 = 8 > 6$.
  Contradiction.
\item \emph{$a \ge 2$, $r = 1$:} $q = 2^a + 1$.  Since $q \ge 13$ we need
  $2^a \ge 12$, so $a \ge 4$.  Then $\tau(n-4) = \tau(2^{a+1}) = a + 2 \ge 6$.
  Equality holds only at $a = 4$, giving $q = 17$ and $n = 36$.  But then
  $n - 1 = 35 = 5 \times 7$ is not prime, contradicting Case~2.
  For $a \ge 5$: $\tau(n-4) \ge 7 > 6$.  Contradiction.
\item \emph{$a = 1$, $r = 1$:} $q = 3$ and $n = 8 \le 24$.  Excluded.
\item \emph{$a = 1$, $r$ composite:} $\tau(r) \ge 3$, so
  $\tau(n-4) = 3\tau(r) \ge 9 > 6$.  Contradiction.
\end{itemize}

The unique surviving sub-case is $a = 1$ with $r$ prime, giving $q = 2r + 1$.
Thus the triple $(r,\, q = 2r+1,\, p = 4r+3)$ consists entirely of primes with
$r \ge 7$ and $n = 4(r+1)$.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 2b: $r \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$ and $3 \mid (n - 3)$.}

By~\eqref{eq:star} with $k = 3$: $\tau(n - 3) \le 5$.

Since $q = 2r + 1 \ge 13$ is prime, $3 \nmid q$, so $2r + 1 \not\equiv 0
\pmod{3}$, i.e.\ $r \not\equiv 1 \pmod{3}$.  Since $r$ is prime and $r \ge 7$,
also $r \not\equiv 0 \pmod{3}$.  Therefore $r \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$, and
$n - 3 = 4r + 1 \equiv 8 + 1 = 9 \equiv 0 \pmod{3}$.

Write $n - 3 = 3s$ with $s = (4r+1)/3$.  We consider the three
possibilities for $s$:

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{$3 \mid s$ (i.e.\ $9 \mid n - 3$):} Write $s = 3t$, so $n - 3 = 9t$.
  For $r \ge 7$: $t = (4r+1)/9 \ge 29/9 > 3$, i.e.\ $t \ge 4$.
  Note also $t \ne 9$: if $t = 9$ then $4r + 1 = 81$, giving $r = 20$, which is
  not prime.  So $t \notin \{1, 3, 9\}$, and $1, 3, 9, t, 3t, 9t$ are
  six distinct divisors of $n - 3$, giving $\tau(n-3) \ge 6 > 5$.
  Contradiction.

\item \emph{$3 \nmid s$, $s$ composite:} Write $s = uv$ with
  $1 < u \le v$ and $3 \nmid s$.  Since $s$ is odd and $3 \nmid s$, every prime
  factor of $s$ is at least $5$, hence $u \ge 5$.  Then
  $1, 3, u, 3u, s, 3s$ are divisors of $n - 3 = 3s$.  We verify they are
  distinct: $1 < 3 < u$ (since $u \ge 5$), $u < 3u$, and $3u < s = uv$ iff
  $3 < v$, which holds since $v \ge u \ge 5$.  Also $s < 3s$.  So
  $\tau(n-3) \ge 6 > 5$.  Contradiction.

\item \emph{$3 \nmid s$, $s$ prime:} This is consistent with $\tau(n - 3) = 4$.
  We continue the analysis below.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 2c: $r \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$, write $r = 3u + 2$,
and introduce $\ell = 2u + 1$.}

With $s$ prime and $r = 3u + 2$ (since $r \equiv 2 \pmod 3$), we compute
$s = (4r + 1)/3 = (12u + 9)/3 = 4u + 3$.
Note $\ell := 2u + 1$ satisfies $s = 2\ell + 1$ and
$r = (3\ell + 1)/2$ (checking: $r = 3u + 2 = 3(\ell - 1)/2 + 2 = (3\ell + 1)/2$~\checkmark).

Also $n - 6 = 4r - 2 = 2(2r - 1)$.  Since $r = 3u + 2$:
$2r - 1 = 6u + 3 = 3(2u + 1) = 3\ell$, so $n - 6 = 6\ell$.

By~\eqref{eq:star} with $k = 6$: $\tau(6\ell) \le 8$.

At this point the following are already established prime:
$r = (3\ell+1)/2$, $q = 3\ell+2$, $p = 6\ell+5$, $s = 2\ell+1$.
The quantity $\ell = 2u+1$ is odd by construction; the sub-case analysis below
will show it must also be prime in any surviving configuration.
(Integrality of $r$ requires $3\ell+1$ even, which holds since $\ell$ is odd.)

We analyse the sub-cases for $\ell$:

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{$3 \mid \ell$, i.e.\ $\ell = 3w$:}
  $n - 6 = 18w$ and $\tau(18w) = \tau(2 \cdot 3^2 \cdot w)$.
  \begin{itemize}
  \item $w = 1$: $\ell = 3$, $u = 1$, $r = 5$, $n = 24$.  Excluded.
  \item $w \ge 2$ even: $4 \mid 18w$, so $\tau(18w) \ge \tau(36) = 9 > 8$.
    Contradiction.
  \item $w \ge 3$ odd with $3 \nmid w$: $\gcd(w, 6) = 1$, so
    $\tau(18w) = \tau(18)\tau(w) = 6\tau(w) \ge 12 > 8$.  Contradiction.
  \item $w \ge 3$ odd with $3 \mid w$, write $w = 3^b e$ with $b \ge 1$, $e$ odd,
    $3 \nmid e$: $\tau(18w) = \tau(2 \cdot 3^{b+2} \cdot e) = 2(b+3)\tau(e)$.
    For $e = 1$: $\tau = 2(b + 3)$.  We need $2(b+3) \le 8$, i.e.\ $b \le 1$,
    so $b = 1$, $w = 3$, $\ell = 9$, $u = 4$, $r = 14$.
    But $r = 14$ is not prime.  Contradiction.
    For $e \ge 3$ (i.e.\ $e$ has a prime factor $\ge 5$):
    $\tau \ge 2\cdot 3 \cdot 2 = 12 > 8$.  Contradiction.
  \end{itemize}
  Every sub-case with $3 \mid \ell$ yields a contradiction.

\item \emph{$3 \nmid \ell$, $\ell$ composite:}
  $\gcd(\ell, 6) = 1$, so $\tau(6\ell) = \tau(6)\tau(\ell) = 4\tau(\ell) \ge 12 > 8$.
  Contradiction.

\item \emph{$3 \nmid \ell$, $\ell$ prime:}
  $\tau(6\ell) = 4 \cdot 2 = 8 \le 8$.  Consistent; we continue.
\end{itemize}

The first two sub-cases are contradictions; the third is the unique survivor.
Hence $\ell$ is prime, and all five of $\ell$, $s$, $r$, $q$, $p$ are now
established prime.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 2d: Using $k = 5$ to pin down $\ell \pmod 5$ and
establish $v$ prime.}

By~\eqref{eq:star} with $k = 5$: $\tau(n - 5) \le 7$.
We have $n - 5 = 4r - 1 = 6\ell + 1$.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Determining $\ell \pmod 5$.}
We show that $\ell \not\equiv 0, 1, 2, 3 \pmod 5$, leaving $\ell \equiv 4$.
Using $2^{-1} \equiv 3 \pmod 5$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\ell \equiv 0$: Then $5 \mid \ell$, so $\ell = 5$ (the only prime
    divisible by $5$). But $r = (3 \cdot 5 + 1)/2 = 8$ is not prime.
    Contradiction.
  \item $\ell \equiv 1$: $q = 3\ell + 2 \equiv 5 \equiv 0 \pmod 5$, so
    $5 \mid q$.  Since $q \ge 13 > 5$, $q$ is composite.  Contradiction.
  \item $\ell \equiv 2$: $s = 2\ell + 1 \equiv 5 \equiv 0 \pmod 5$, so
    $5 \mid s$.  Since $s \ge 11 > 5$, $s$ is composite.  Contradiction.
  \item $\ell \equiv 3$: $r = (3\ell+1)/2 \equiv (9+1) \cdot 3 = 30
    \equiv 0 \pmod 5$.  So $5 \mid r$; since $r$ is prime, $r = 5$.
    But $r \ge 7$.  Contradiction.
\end{itemize}
Hence $\ell \equiv 4 \pmod 5$.  Then $6\ell + 1 \equiv 25 \equiv 0 \pmod 5$,
so write $n - 5 = 5v$ with $v = (6\ell + 1)/5 \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}$.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{$v$ is odd.}
Since $r$ is an odd prime ($r \ge 7$), $4r \equiv 0 \pmod 4$, so
$n - 5 = 4r - 1 \equiv 3 \pmod 4$.  Thus $5v \equiv 3 \pmod 4$, and
since $5 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, we get $v \equiv 3 \pmod 4$.  In particular
$v$ is odd.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{$v$ is prime.}
Write $\ell = 5j + 4$; then $v = (6(5j+4)+1)/5 = 6j + 5$.  So
$5 \mid v \iff 5 \mid 6j + 5 \iff 5 \mid 6j \iff 5 \mid j$.

\emph{Case $5 \nmid v$:}  $\tau(5v) = 2\tau(v) \le 7$, so $\tau(v) \le 3$.
If $\tau(v) = 3$ then $v = p^2$ for some odd prime $p$, but $p^2 \equiv 1
\pmod 4$ for any odd $p$, contradicting $v \equiv 3 \pmod 4$.
If $\tau(v) = 1$ then $v = 1$, giving $n = 6 \le 24$, excluded.
Hence $\tau(v) = 2$ and $v$ is prime.

\emph{Case $5 \mid v$, i.e.\ $5 \mid j$:}
Write $v = 5^c v'$ with $c \ge 1$ and $\gcd(v', 5) = 1$.  Then
$\tau(5v) = (c + 2)\tau(v') \le 7$.
We enumerate the possibilities $(c, \tau(v'))$:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $c = 1$, $\tau(v') = 1$ (so $v' = 1$, $v = 5$):
    $6\ell + 1 = 25$, giving $\ell = 4$, which is not prime.  Contradiction.
  \item $c = 1$, $\tau(v') = 2$ (so $v'$ is prime, $v = 5v'$):
    $6\ell + 1 = 25v'$, so $\ell = (25v' - 1)/6$.
    Since $5 \mid j$ and $\ell = 5j + 4$, we have $\ell \equiv 4 \pmod{25}$.
    Combined with $\ell \equiv 2 \pmod 3$, CRT over $\mathrm{lcm}(3,25) = 75$
    gives $\ell \equiv 29 \pmod{75}$.

    We derive a contradiction using only the already-established primes
    $r$, $q$, $p$, $s$ (no assumption on $v$).
    Writing $\ell = 75k + 29$ for some non-negative integer $k$,
    we have $\ell \equiv 5k + 1 \pmod 7$ (since $75 \equiv 5 \pmod 7$).
    The six residues $\ell \pmod 7$ arise as $k$ ranges over residues mod $7$:
    \begin{itemize}
      \item $\ell \equiv 0 \pmod 7$ ($k \equiv 4$): Then $7 \mid \ell$;
        since $\ell$ is prime, $\ell = 7$.  But $\ell \equiv 2 \pmod 3$
        (Step~2b) and $7 \equiv 1 \pmod 3 \ne 2$.  Contradiction.
      \item $\ell \equiv 1 \pmod 7$ ($k \equiv 0$): $k = 7k'$, so $\ell = 525k' + 29$.
        The smallest case is $k'=0$, $\ell = 29$.  Then $r = (3\cdot29+1)/2 = 44$,
        not prime — contradiction for $k'=0$.  For general $k'$:
        $r = (3\ell+1)/2 \equiv (3\cdot1+1)\cdot4 = 16 \equiv 2 \pmod 7$
        (using $2^{-1}\equiv 4$), so $7\nmid r$.
        However, $v' = (6\ell+1)/25 \equiv (6\cdot1+1)\cdot2 = 14\equiv 0\pmod7$
        (using $25^{-1}\equiv 2\pmod7$), so $7\mid v'$.  Since $v'$ is prime,
        $v'=7$, giving $\ell = (175-1)/6 = 29$, and $r=44$: not prime.
        Contradiction.
      \item $\ell \equiv 2 \pmod 7$ ($k \equiv 3$): $r\equiv(6+1)\cdot4=28\equiv0\pmod7$,
        so $7\mid r$; since $r$ prime and $r\ge7$, $r=7$, giving $3\ell+1=14$,
        $\ell=13/3$: not an integer.  Contradiction.
      \item $\ell \equiv 3 \pmod 7$ ($k \equiv 6$): $s=2\ell+1\equiv7\equiv0\pmod7$;
        $s$ prime and $s\ge11>7$: impossible.  Contradiction.
      \item $\ell \equiv 4 \pmod 7$ ($k \equiv 2$): $q=3\ell+2\equiv14\equiv0\pmod7$;
        $q$ prime and $q\ge17>7$: impossible.  Contradiction.
      \item $\ell \equiv 5 \pmod 7$ ($k \equiv 1$): $p=6\ell+5\equiv35\equiv0\pmod7$;
        $p$ prime and $p\ge35>7$: impossible.  Contradiction.
      \item $\ell \equiv 6 \pmod 7$ ($k \equiv 5$): We verify explicitly.
        Using $2^{-1}\equiv4$ and $5^{-1}\equiv3\pmod7$:
        $r\equiv19\cdot4=76\equiv6$, $q\equiv20\equiv6$, $p\equiv41\equiv6$,
        $s\equiv13\equiv6\pmod7$.  None of $r, q, p, s$ is divisible by $7$.
        We have $\ell\equiv2\pmod3$, $\ell\equiv4\pmod5$, $\ell\equiv6\pmod7$;
        since $6\equiv-1$, $2\equiv-1$, $4\equiv-1$ modulo $7,3,5$ respectively,
        CRT over $105$ gives $\ell\equiv104\pmod{105}$.
        Since $\ell$ is an odd prime, $\ell=105k+104$ is odd, which forces
        $k$ to be odd.  Writing $k=2m+1$ gives $\ell=210m+209$, i.e.\
        $\ell\equiv209\pmod{210}$.  Since $210\equiv1\pmod{11}$, we get
        $\ell\equiv209\equiv0\pmod{11}$.  So $11\mid\ell$; since $\ell$ is
        prime, $\ell=11$.  But $11\equiv1\pmod5$, while $\ell\equiv4\pmod5$.
        Contradiction.
    \end{itemize}
    Every residue of $\ell \pmod 7$ yields a contradiction.
  \item $c = 2$, $\tau(v') = 1$ (so $v' = 1$, $v = 25$):
    $6\ell + 1 = 125$, so $\ell = 124/6$, which is not an integer.
    Contradiction.
  \item $c = 3$, $\tau(v') = 1$ (so $v = 125$):
    $\ell = (625 - 1)/6 = 104 = 8 \times 13$, not prime.  Contradiction.
  \item $c \ge 2$, $\tau(v') \ge 2$: $(c+2)\tau(v') \ge 4 \cdot 2 = 8 > 7$.
    Contradiction.
  \item $c \ge 4$, $\tau(v') = 1$ (so $v = 5^c$):
    $c + 2 \ge 6$, but $\tau(5v) = c + 2 \le 7$ forces $c \le 5$.
    $c = 4$: $\ell = (5^5 - 1)/6 = 3124/6$, non-integer.
    $c = 5$: $\ell = (5^6 - 1)/6 = 2604 = 4 \times 651$, not prime.
    Contradiction.
\end{itemize}
Every sub-case with $5 \mid v$ yields an immediate contradiction.

Therefore $5 \nmid v$, and $v$ is prime.

All six quantities $\ell$, $s = 2\ell+1$, $r = (3\ell+1)/2$, $q = 3\ell+2$,
$p = 6\ell+5$, $v = (6\ell+1)/5$ are now established prime.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Step 2e: Final contradiction via residues mod $7$ and $11$.}

We now have all of
\begin{equation}
  \ell,\quad s = 2\ell + 1,\quad r = \tfrac{3\ell+1}{2},\quad
  q = 3\ell + 2,\quad p = 6\ell + 5,\quad v = \tfrac{6\ell+1}{5}
\end{equation}
prime, with $\ell$ an odd prime, $\ell \equiv 2 \pmod 3$, $\ell \equiv 4 \pmod 5$,
and $\ell \ge 5$.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Modulo $7$.}  We show each residue $\ell \pmod 7$ forces one
of the six prime quantities to equal $7$ (yielding a contradiction) or
requires $\ell$ to be non-integer (impossible).  Recall: $\ell \ge 5$,
$s = 2\ell+1 \ge 11$, $r \ge 7$, $q \ge 17$, $p \ge 35$, and
$v = (6\ell+1)/5 > 6$ so $v \ge 7$.
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\ell \equiv 0 \pmod 7$: Then $7 \mid \ell$.  Since $\ell$ is prime,
    $\ell = 7$.  But Step~2b established $\ell \equiv 2 \pmod 3$, and
    $7 \equiv 1 \pmod 3 \ne 2$.  Contradiction.
  \item $\ell \equiv 1 \pmod 7$: $v \equiv (6\cdot 1+1)\cdot 3 = 21 \equiv 0
    \pmod 7$ (using $5^{-1} \equiv 3 \pmod 7$), so $7 \mid v$.  Since $v$ is
    prime and $v \ge 7$, we get $v = 7$, giving $6\ell + 1 = 35$,
    i.e.\ $\ell = 34/6$: not an integer.  Contradiction.
  \item $\ell \equiv 2 \pmod 7$: $r \equiv (6+1)\cdot 4 = 28 \equiv 0 \pmod 7$
    (using $2^{-1} \equiv 4 \pmod 7$), so $7 \mid r$.  Since $r$ is prime and
    $r \ge 7$, we get $r = 7$, giving $3\ell + 1 = 14$, i.e.\ $\ell = 13/3$:
    not an integer.  Contradiction.
  \item $\ell \equiv 3 \pmod 7$: $s = 2\ell+1 \equiv 7 \equiv 0 \pmod 7$, so
    $7 \mid s$.  Since $s$ is prime and $s \ge 11 > 7$, this is impossible.
    Contradiction.
  \item $\ell \equiv 4 \pmod 7$: $q = 3\ell+2 \equiv 14 \equiv 0 \pmod 7$, so
    $7 \mid q$.  Since $q$ is prime and $q \ge 17 > 7$, this is impossible.
    Contradiction.
  \item $\ell \equiv 5 \pmod 7$: $p = 6\ell+5 \equiv 35 \equiv 0 \pmod 7$, so
    $7 \mid p$.  Since $p$ is prime and $p \ge 35 > 7$, this is impossible.
    Contradiction.
  \item $\ell \equiv 6 \pmod 7$: We verify that none of the six quantities is
    divisible by $7$.  Using $2^{-1} \equiv 4$ and $5^{-1} \equiv 3 \pmod 7$:
    \[
      \ell \equiv 6,\quad
      s = 2\ell+1 \equiv 13 \equiv 6,\quad
      r = \tfrac{3\ell+1}{2} \equiv 19\cdot 4 = 76 \equiv 6,
    \]
    \[
      q = 3\ell+2 \equiv 20 \equiv 6,\quad
      p = 6\ell+5 \equiv 41 \equiv 6,\quad
      v = \tfrac{6\ell+1}{5} \equiv 37\cdot 3 = 111 \equiv 6 \pmod 7.
    \]
    None is divisible by $7$, so mod-$7$ yields no contradiction in this
    case.
\end{itemize}

Since every residue class $\ell \equiv 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 \pmod 7$ has been
eliminated, we are forced to conclude $\ell \equiv 6 \pmod 7$.
Combined with $\ell \equiv 2 \pmod 3$ (established in Step~2b) and
$\ell \equiv 4 \pmod 5$ (established in Step~2d), all three congruences hold
simultaneously in this surviving branch.  Since $6 \equiv -1$, $2 \equiv -1$,
$4 \equiv -1$ modulo $7$, $3$, $5$ respectively, CRT over $3 \times 5 \times 7
= 105$ gives $\ell \equiv -1 \pmod{105}$, i.e.\ $\ell \equiv 104 \pmod{105}$.

\smallskip
\noindent\emph{Final contradiction via mod~$11$.}
Since $\ell$ is an odd prime, write $\ell = 105k + 104$.  For $\ell$ to be odd,
we need $105k + 104$ odd; since $105$ is odd, this requires $k$ to be odd.
Setting $k = 2m + 1$ gives $\ell = 210m + 209$, i.e.\ $\ell \equiv 209
\pmod{210}$.  Now $210 = 19 \cdot 11 + 1 \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$, so
$\ell \equiv 209 \equiv 209 - 19 \cdot 11 = 0 \pmod{11}$.  Hence $11 \mid \ell$.
Since $\ell$ is prime, $\ell = 11$.  But $11 \equiv 1 \pmod 5$, while Step~2d
established $\ell \equiv 4 \pmod 5$.  Contradiction.

This eliminates the final case, so every branch leads to a contradiction.
Therefore no $n > 24$ satisfies the inequality.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Verification for $n = 24$.}

The complete table of $m + \tau(m)$ for $1 \le m \le 23$ is:
\[
\begin{array}{r|r|r||r|r|r||r|r|r}
m & \tau(m) & m{+}\tau & m & \tau(m) & m{+}\tau & m & \tau(m) & m{+}\tau\\\hline
1 & 1 & 2  & 9  & 3 & 12 & 17 & 2 & 19\\
2 & 2 & 4  & 10 & 4 & 14 & 18 & 6 & 24\\
3 & 2 & 5  & 11 & 2 & 13 & 19 & 2 & 21\\
4 & 3 & 7  & 12 & 6 & 18 & 20 & 6 & 26\\
5 & 2 & 7  & 13 & 2 & 15 & 21 & 4 & 25\\
6 & 4 & 10 & 14 & 4 & 18 & 22 & 4 & 26\\
7 & 2 & 9  & 15 & 4 & 19 & 23 & 2 & 25\\
8 & 4 & 12 & 16 & 5 & 21 & & &
\end{array}
\]
The maximum value is $26$, achieved at $m = 20$ and $m = 22$, giving
$\max_{1 \le m \le 23}(m + \tau(m)) = 26 = 24 + 2$.
Hence $n = 24$ satisfies the inequality, completing the proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The key steps are: (i) $n$ must be even; (ii) $n - 2 = 2q$, $n - 1 = p$,
$q = 2r + 1$, all prime (a Cunningham chain); (iii) writing $r = 3u+2$, setting
$\ell = 2u+1$ reduces the problem; (iv) $\ell \equiv 4 \pmod 5$ from a divisor
count involving $n - 5$; (v) $\ell \equiv 6$ modulo each of $3, 5, 7$ gives
$\ell \equiv 104 \pmod{105}$; (vi) since $\ell$ is an odd prime,
$\ell \equiv 209 \pmod{210}$, but then $11 \mid \ell$ (as $209 \equiv 0
\pmod{11}$ and $210 \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$), so $\ell = 11$; but $11 \equiv 1
\pmod 5$ while $\ell \equiv 4 \pmod 5$ --- contradiction.
The proof is thus fully elementary, using only divisor-count estimates,
modular arithmetic, and the Chinese Remainder Theorem.
\end{remark}
